What's Left After Big Stars Burn Out?

Neutron Stars

After a star 4 to 8 times larger than the Sun expends the rest of its fuel and goes through a supernova, the core that is left behind is a neutron star. Atoms, the fundamental building blocks of matter, normally have a nucleus of positively charged protons, with negatively-charged electrons orbiting that nucleus and a lot of empty space in between the two. Within a neutron star, however, the gravitational pull is so strong that it crushes atoms, eliminating all the empty space between nuclei. Protons and electrons are forced together, equalizing each other and forming neutrons. Because of this, neutron stars tend to only be about the size of a city, but are so dense that a teaspoon's worth of their matter weighs millions of tons. They radiate intense energy via X-rays, and have surface temperatures of over 1 million K.

Image: Artist's conception of a neutron star.

Pulsars

Sometimes a neutron star will have so much energy left to expel that it is reclassified as a pulsar. Pulsars spin at several hundred revolutions per second, and emit huge bursts of gamma rays and X-rays at their poles. Scientists observe them by their "pulses" - the rays emitted when their poles are pointing at Earth - and are still learning why they emit rays in this way.

Image: Artist's conception of a pulsar emitting gamma rays.

Black Holes

If a star is 10 times larger than the Sun or greater, it may become a neutron star at the end of its life, but will likely become so dense that it will completely collapse inward on itself to form a black hole. Black holes are incredibly dense, at least a few times as dense as the Sun, and have such powerful gravity that even light cannot escape them. They consume all matter around them and continue to grow. Some black holes are supermassive, with billions of times the mass of the Sun. It is theorized that supermassive black holes reside at the heart of most galaxies, but scientists are not sure how these giants become so large.

Image: Artist's conception of a black hole absorbing matter. Black holes are so dense they distort the space around them.

Black holes have two major parts: an event horizon and a singularity. The event horizon around a black hole is the radius at which light cannot escape it. The singularity is its central point, where its mass is concentrated.
