The Life Cycle of a Star

What Are Stars?

Stars are giant balls of ionized gas, or plasma, that convert hydrogen into helium through nuclear fusion at millions of degrees. They are in constant balance between their intense gravity, which holds them together, and the energy released from fusion, which tries to force them apart. The hottest stars appear blue; relatively cooler stars appear orange or red. Stars can be many thousands of times larger than our Sun, and can have surface temperatures ranging from 4,000K (Kelvin) to 50,000K (roughly the same in degrees Celsius), as well as a core temperature of 10 to 15 million K.

Image: The Sun is a comparatively small star, but perfect for life on Earth.

Birth of a Star

Stars are "born" in nebulae, clouds of hydrogen gas and dust in space. As the gas condenses it exerts a greater gravitational force on other debris. As more debris and gas collapse inward, their collisions begin to generate heat and light, and they become protostars. If enough matter is collected, and if temperatures approach 10 million K, hydrogen atoms will begin to fuse into helium. At this point, huge amounts of energy are released, the gas stops collapsing inward, and the now-equalized Main Sequence star begins to shine.

Image: The Rosette Nebula. The bright stars in the center are very young, at only a few million years old.

Star Life

A Main Sequence star the size of our Sun will burn consistently for about 10 billion years. Larger stars may only last a few billion or even only a few million years. As it runs out of hydrogen, the core of the star will contract and its outer shell will expand and cool. The star has become a red giant (or in some cases a hotter, larger blue giant), and may remain so for a few million years, fusing helium into carbon. To put it in perspective, when the Sun reaches this stage, it will completely envelop Mercury and Venus!

Image: Our sun has a few billion years of fuel left before it becomes a red giant, like Arcturus.

Death

When a star the mass of our Sun reaches the end of its red giant stage, it will completely lose its balance and shed most of its mass in a nova. Its hydrogen and helium is thrown outward; this gas forms a planetary nebula from which new stars can be formed. The remainder of the star, its core and a thin layer of the outer shell, will still shine for many years and is called a white dwarf. When white dwarfs expend the rest of their fuel, they finally "die," stop glowing, and become black dwarfs.

Image: The Cat's Eye planetary nebula.
The center of the "eye" is the creator of the nebula, now a white dwarf.

If a star has a mass many times larger than that of the Sun, the end of its life may be much more spectacular. Massive stars can grow well past red giant size until they become red supergiants, with temperatures at their cores approaching 600 million K! Heavier and heavier elements are fused together in the core at this temperature until the core is mostly iron; at this point, gravity finally wins, pulling the outer shell of gas in and ricocheting it out in a massive explosion called a supernova.
The old core of the star may remain as a neutron star, or may collapse in on itself to form a black hole - learn more about them on the next page!
