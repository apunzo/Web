Search For Alien Life

Exoplanets

Exoplanets are planets that orbit around other stars, and their research is vital to the potential discovery of extraterrestrial life. At first, scientists believed that all planets form according to the core accretion theory, which perfectly depicts the formation of our solar system as we hypothesized. The theory describes our system starting out as a thick disc of gas and dust accumulating around the newly-formed Sun. Over millions of years, the disc solidified into the rocky, small inner planets and the massive, gaseous outer planets. This theory worked well until we became able to see other planetary systems, in which it seems that exoplanets and other stars can form in any way physically possible. We learned that our solar system demonstrates just one mode of formation, and that our system is unusual in the number of planets orbiting the Sun. This meant other Earth-like planets, that are potentially ideal for extraterrestrial life (if the life is similar to that on Earth), would have to be closely observed in how they are formed.

SETI

SETI, or Search for Extra-Terrestrial Intelligence, is one of several organizations dedicated to finding alien life. They scan the heavens with arrays of satellites, searching for radio or other signals that are not generated naturally. They also search for evidence of conditions on exoplanets that would be suitable for single-celled life, such as water and temperature ranges within the bounds found on Earth. SETI is always looking for ways to beat the odds and use technology to traverse rough terrain on Earth in order to practice for potential excursions on Venus, Mars, and Jupiter's moons.
